Winter

THINGS TO CONSIDER
Let the local USDA Hardiness Zone guide your winter gardening: This will prep you on which crops to grow that won’t require frost protection.Always keep an eye out for any open planting space during late winter, after harvesting your winter crops. Late winter crops can typically be harvested in early spring for optimal yield and soil use.


WHEN TO PLANT
Plant winter crops in late summer to early fall for a winter harvest.

WHEN TO HARVEST
Harvest times are crop-dependent. This includes type and time-to-yield of that crop.

Spring

THINGS TO CONSIDER
Raising your garden beads will expand the growing area while also helping in crop growth.To get your spring soil ready for planting, be sure to enrich it with as much organic material as possible. Use a 1-inch layer of this material around the based of each plant to keep the soil moist and prohibit weed growth.


WHEN TO PLANT
Plant cool-season vegetables before the last frost date and warm-season crops after that date. Sowing times are determined by the type of veggie you’re planting and the weather in your area.

WHEN TO HARVEST
The proper time to harvest your vegetables depends on the crops you sow, but most spring veggies mature between May and September.

Summer

THINGS TO CONSIDER
Diversify your crops so that you won’t have as many problems with pests. You may want to consider disease resistant vegetation.To avoid overwhelming yourself, focus on one picking at a time. If you set up your seed beds and mulch well, you'll cut down on wasted time weeding; but wind up harvesting crops that produce well and come in at the same time.


WHEN TO PLANT
Summer veggies should be planted in the spring after the last frost. Most warm climate crops cannot survive cold weather and require temperatures between 65 to 90 degrees.

WHEN TO HARVEST
Summer crops produce late fall harvests; the amount of time needed for maturity depends on the type of vegetable that’s planted.

Autumn

THINGS TO CONSIDER
How you plan out your fall crops depends on regional climate. Hot weather requires you to start fall crops indoors before moving them outdoors when the temperature drops.Refresh your garden bed as you harvest summer crops for fall planting. Use an organic fertilizer or small amount of compost, and consider a cold-hardy crop for that spot as a preemptive precaution against colder weather.


WHEN TO PLANT
Timing is critical for a successful autumn garden. Know your region's first fall frost date and plan accordingly.

WHEN TO HARVEST
The number of days needed to fulfil a harvest depends on the crops you've planted. Some crops produce during the fall months; others, late winter or early spring.
